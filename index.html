The Suez Canal is a man-made waterway in Egypt connecting the Mediterranean Sea and the Red Sea.

Map of Seuz Canal

History

  • The idea of connecting the Mediterranean and Red Seas dates back to ancient times, with smaller canals existing as early as 2000 BC.
  • The modern Suez Canal's construction began in 1859 and it officially opened on November 17, 1869.
  • It was built by the Universal Suez Ship Canal Company, a French-led venture.
  • The canal became strategically vital, leading to British acquisition of a major share in 1875 and eventual control after the 1882 invasion of Egypt.
  • The 1888 Convention of Constantinople declared it a neutral zone, open to ships of all nations during peace and war.
  • Egyptian President Gamal Abdel Nasser nationalized the canal in July 1956, sparking the Suez Crisis.
  • The canal was closed several times due to conflicts, notably after the 1967 Arab-Israeli War until 1975.

Importance

  • The Suez Canal is the shortest sea link between East and West.
  • It eliminates the need for ships to travel around Africa's Cape of Good Hope, significantly reducing travel distance and time. For example, the journey from the Arabian Sea to London is shortened by approximately 8,900 kilometers (5,500 miles).
  • This results in considerable savings in fuel consumption and ship operating costs.
  • It's a crucial artery for global trade, with about 12-15% of world trade and around 30% of global container traffic passing through it annually.
  • It is particularly important for the transport of oil and liquefied natural gas from the Middle East to Europe and North America.
  • The Suez Canal is a major source of revenue for Egypt.
